Content Style Guide & Voice

How we write for Matt. Smart Brevity format, his vocabulary, his voice. The rules for anyone creating BLI content.

Smart Brevity: The format

Why this matters: Matt's audience is senior leaders. They're overloaded (his whole thesis). If we write content that adds to their overload, we've failed before we started. Smart Brevity respects their time — and models the Pause & Reflect discipline Matt teaches.

Rule 1

Lead with the strongest sentence.

No throat-clearing. No "In today's fast-paced world." The first sentence IS the point.

In today's rapidly changing business landscape, leaders are increasingly finding themselves overwhelmed by the pace of change and the demands placed on their time, attention, and decision-making capacity.
Capacity is the defining leadership challenge of the AI era. The always-on pace overloads us, draining the mental and emotional bandwidth to process what comes our way.
Rule 2

"Why it matters" — bolded, every time.

After the lead, tell the reader why they should care. Bold the label. This is the Smart Brevity signature move.

Why it matters: When load exceeds capacity, our brains default to System 1 binary thinking — false choices, either/or shortcuts, us/them narratives. That's the Binary Trap. Scaled to organizations, it produces the Four Taxes: False Choices, Silos, Short-Termism, and Stalled Innovation.
Rule 3

One idea per paragraph. Short paragraphs.

If a paragraph makes two points, it's two paragraphs. Three sentences max. If you can say it in one, do.

Rule 4

"The big picture" or "Go deeper" for context.

If there's nuance or background, put it under a bolded label at the end. Don't bury it in the lead.

Go deeper: The Binary Trap & the Causal Chain are a tension pair. The trap is what happens — overload collapses thinking into either/or. The chain is why — the predictable sequence from accelerating change to organizational dysfunction. Matt holds them together because that's how the Power of & works.
Rule 5

End with one clear action.

Every piece of content ends with one thing the reader can do. Not three. One.

Where Smart Brevity applies — and where it doesn't

USE Smart Brevity for:
Blog posts on bluelakeideas.com
LinkedIn posts
Substack essays
YouTube descriptions
Email campaigns (Overloaded waitlist)
Any draft content for Matt to review
Internal strategy docs for the team
DON'T use Smart Brevity for:

JSON-LD schema markup — AI engines need complete, information-dense sentences to cite. Fragments don't get pulled as AI answers. The FAQ schema needs to read like a knowledgeable person answering a question, not a bullet point.

Meta descriptions — These are 150–160 characters of keyword-rich copy optimized for search results, not readability. They're already brief by constraint but they serve Google, not the reader.

Pillar pages — Google rewards depth. A 300-word Smart Brevity post won't outrank a 1,500-word comprehensive page for a head term like "what is & intelligence." The pillar page needs to be the most thorough source on the internet for that topic. Write it in Smart Brevity style (short paragraphs, bolded labels) but don't cut the depth.

The bottom line: Smart Brevity on the surface for humans. SEO depth underneath for machines. Both layers matter.

Matt's voice: How he sounds

The big picture: Matt's voice is warm, direct, and grounded in experience. He's not an academic — he's a practitioner who's lived it. He doesn't lecture. He shares. He's the executive who's been in the room, made the hard call, and found a better way.

Warm but authoritative

He's not soft. He challenges audiences. But he does it with empathy, not ego. He's been where they are.

Paradox is the native mode

Matt thinks in &s, not either/ors. Every concept has a tension partner. The Binary Trap & The Causal Chain. Clarity & Courage. Personal & professional. If a piece of content presents only one side, it's not Matt's voice.

Experience over theory

He cites Collins, references neuroscience, draws from Kahneman. But the authority comes from "I led teams across nine states" and "I faced burnout at the peak of my career." Lead with the lived experience, support with the research.

The mantra test

Matt's strongest content can be reduced to one sentence someone remembers. "I can do anything. But not everything." "The & HAS to be true." "Capacity is the defining leadership challenge of the AI era." If a draft doesn't have a sentence like that, keep writing until it does.

Blog post template (Smart Brevity + SEO)

Why it matters: This is the format for every blog post on bluelakeideas.com. Smart Brevity for the reader. SEO structure for Google and AI.

Structure

Headline: Clear, keyword-front-loaded, under 65 characters. Include the named concept.
Example: "The Four Taxes of Binary Thinking"

Lead (1–2 sentences): The point. No preamble.
"Binary thinking doesn't just limit leaders — it taxes entire organizations. There are four."

Why it matters (1 paragraph): Bolded label. Stakes. What's at risk.
"Why it matters: When overload triggers the Binary Trap, organizations pay four compounding costs..."

Body (3–5 short sections): One idea per section. Bolded sub-headers. Short paragraphs. Use Matt's exact vocabulary from the canonical terms list.

Go deeper (optional): Background, research, nuance. For readers who want more.

One CTA: End with one action.
"Matt's forthcoming book Overloaded goes deep on the Binary Trap & the Causal Chain. Join the waitlist →"

The SEO layer underneath: After the blog post is written in Smart Brevity, add FAQ schema (JSON-LD) to the page with 2–3 questions the post answers. These should be full, information-dense sentences — not Smart Brevity fragments. The blog post is for humans. The schema is for machines. Both publish on the same page.
